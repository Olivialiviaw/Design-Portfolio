Context

Credit unions sit on a goldmine of member data — job changes, equity thresholds, rate-shopping signals — that goes largely unread until it's too late to act. Loan officers miss windows. VPs see numbers after decisions have already been made elsewhere.

's premise: what if the data could surface its own urgency? I designed the Analytics module — the executive layer where signal performance, LO accountability, and AI-generated recommendations coexist without overwhelming anyone.

Problem

Credit union leadership has data. What they don't have is the time, context, or interface to turn it into action.

VP-level users aren't analysts. They need to walk into a screen, understand what requires attention right now, and leave with a decision made. Every extra click is friction — and friction costs loans.

01
Static reports arrive too late. By the time a VP sees a funnel drop-off, the member is already at a competitor. Reporting is retrospective; the decision needed to happen days earlier.
02
AI intelligence was buried. Signal insights were tucked in a sidebar, treated as a footnote rather than the primary value driver. Most users didn't know it was there.
03
"Accountability" was the wrong frame. It implies blame. What leadership needed was coaching visibility — who needs support, not who failed.
04
Information density without hierarchy. Everything had the same visual weight. A VP scanning for urgent signals had no entry point.
How might we —
Surface the 2–3 things a VP needs to act on today, without requiring them to dig through everything else?
Make AI feel like a layer of the interface — not a chatbot appended to the corner?
Shift from accountability to intelligence — from reporting what happened to recommending what to do next?
The deliverable wasn't a report page. It was a decision center — the place where a VP either acts, or knows exactly why they shouldn't.

Iteration

Reviews after the first hi-fi surfaced a clear pattern: the structure was sound, but nothing told you what to look at first. AI was invisible. The language put people on the defensive. Three rounds of targeted iteration fixed each of those.

New additions

Signal Deep Dive and Performance Analytics were net-new designs — not revisions of an existing page. These are the decisions that defined them.

Signal Deep Dive
Per-signal intelligence — from volume trend to campaign linkage
  • KPI strip with two primary KPIs unified in one gradient-bordered frame — visual pairing makes the relationship legible
  • SVG trend chart: horizontal gradient fill, drop shadow filter, live Volume / Conversion toggle
  • Insight Summary: four AI-generated cards (Top Segment / Underperforming Stage / Revenue Driver / Risk Indicator) — each with gradient border + semantic dot
  • Product breakdown table + bar chart side-by-side
  • Linked campaign performance: see exactly which outreach is converting from this signal
Performance Analytics
Renamed from "Accountability" — coaching posture, not surveillance
  • The rename was the most important design decision. Same data — completely different relationship between the viewer and the page
  • Alert rows: collapsible, severity-tagged (Critical / Watch), each with a Recommended Action and direct CTA button
  • Campaign ROI table with AI dot badge marking AI-analyzed performance data
  • LO performance rows: color-coded status dots + pills (Excellent / Good / Needs Attention)
  • Chip-group filter: All Alerts / Loan Officer / Campaign / Pipeline
AI Intelligence Page
Executive summary — the whole business in one scroll
  • AI-generated weekly summary: 3–5 sentence narrative, main drivers, what changed
  • Risk Radar: current drop-offs, low-ROI campaigns, underperforming segments with quantified impact
  • Opportunity Signals: high-converting segments, revenue acceleration areas, recommended allocation shift
  • Full Ask AI module: input, 4 preset data-grounded questions, AI response with supporting stats, question history
AI Visual Language System
Amber → cyan gradient as the exclusive AI signal
  • Floating AI button: fixed right edge, doesn't scroll, always accessible, one per page
  • Gradient border on AI-powered KPIs and insight cards — same language, consistent application
  • Recommended action indicators: gradient left-bar instead of solid teal
  • Ask AI input: gradient focus ring on interaction
  • Rule: gradient used everywhere AI applies, nowhere it doesn't — making AI legible as a design decision, not a feature
Key Decisions

KPI numbers scaled dramatically. A VP scans numbers first, reads labels second. When the number is the same size as the label, nothing feels urgent.

Ask AI onto the main canvas. Full-width block, four preset questions grounded in this week's actual data — not generic prompts. Analyst briefing, not chatbot.

Liquid-glass navigation. Sidebar and topbar with backdrop-blur. Data cards stayed opaque — glass only where you feel the layer, not where you need to read clearly.

Fixed floating AI button. Always accessible. Doesn't scroll. One entry point per page. The lowest-friction path to intelligence.

Looking back

The most important design decision on this project wasn't visual — it was strategic. I realized early that my contribution would need to carry the module independently. With a team dynamic where the execution largely fell to me, I had to become fluent in parts of the craft I hadn't planned on: design systems discipline, component logic, interaction behavior, and the kind of writing-through-design that makes a VP feel like the interface actually understands their job.

That constraint turned out to be a gift. Owning something completely — without diffusion of responsibility — sharpens your thinking in ways that collaboration sometimes softens. Every decision was mine to make and mine to defend. Over seven weeks, I got very good at defending them.

I also learned something more durable: where AI belongs in an enterprise tool. Not as a chatbot in the corner — as a typographic layer. A signal that this information was synthesized for you, not merely retrieved. The gradient accent system became exactly that: a design language for intelligence, applied everywhere it applies and nowhere it doesn't.

The gap between what a dashboard shows and what a leader can actually do with it — that's the design problem I want to keep working on.
